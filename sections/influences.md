# Influences

This document operationalizes for AI-coupled practice the methodological traditions developed in adjacent fields over decades. The works named below shaped its structure and vocabulary; this section identifies them without summarizing them. Deeper engagement with each is the work of subsequent versions.

**Naturalistic decision-making (Gary Klein).** Klein's work on how experts recognize patterns and act on them under uncertainty informs Section V's treatment of failure shapes: shapes are recognizable patterns the practitioner learns to detect early, not catalogued mistakes to memorize.

**Distributed and extended cognition (Edwin Hutchins; Andy Clark).** Hutchins' framing of cognition as a property of human-tool-environment systems, and Clark's extended-mind thesis (cognition extended into the tools an agent uses), inform the methodology's treatment of operator-AI work as a coupled cognitive system rather than a tool-use relationship.

**Cognitive load theory and worked-example research (John Sweller, Alexander Renkl).** This tradition informs the forward-looking annex's specimen-and-narrative pedagogy: detailed examples carry more transfer value than abstract principles for practitioners learning a new discipline.

**The reflective practitioner (Donald Schön).** Schön's distinction between reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action informs Section II's Verify and Record steps: verifying happens during the work; recording happens after, and both are required for compound learning.

**Deliberate practice (K. Anders Ericsson).** Ericsson's research on expert performance informs Section VIII's framing of compound practice: practice that compounds is structured around feedback and oriented toward specific weaknesses identified by capture.

These works do not provide citations for individual claims in this document. They establish the broader methodological context within which the operator-AI work treated here can be understood.
